Advanced Course in Engineering, Summer 2018
This 10 week long intensive cyber security boot camp comprised of three distinct parts: weekly challenges, research, tradecrafts, and network administrative challenges. This program is under the United States Air Force Research Lab (AFRL) and AIS. Over these ten weeks, I became experienced with UNIX and Powershell.

Weekly Challenges

Every week, we were introduced to a new topic, attended a day lecture on this topic, and was given a challenge problem related to the topic on hand. After each week, we had to present on our solution and write a 10+ page paper on the solution.

Week 1: Systems Requirements Specifications
I was tasked with writing a proper System Requirements Specification paper for my research. A System Requirements Specification is important to any sort of development. It allows developers to pinpoint and orient their goals and objectives for a specific project. It focuses on the scope of the project, the purpose of the project, the intended users of the project, and the functional and non-functional requirements of the project. It heavily emphasizes use cases for the project.

Week 2: Concept of Operations
I was tasked with creating a concept of operations for Air Force missions. The solution is based on the doctine Joint Publication 3-50 Personnel Recovery . I utilized the System-Theoretic Process Analysis (STPA) of System-Theoretic and Technical Operational Management (STORM). The solution required access control logic.

Week 3: Secure Enclave Design
The weekly challenge of this week involved infiltrating and finding sensitive information through a network. This required the use of a reverse shell which allowed us to perform command injection. We had to infiltrate three target machines with each target machine holding a unique circumstance that we must take into consideration when gaining access to each machine. For example, one machine only allowed access through port 443.

Week 4: Network Reconnassiance
This weekly challenge was similar to the previous week in that we had to infiltrate a network for sensitive information. We were only provided an IP address and a port number. This network was connected to a few other networks that we had to access through the esclation of privleges. I performed active and passive network reconnaissance and found different domains. I used tools such as a Remote Desktop Protocol (RDP), command injection, Hot Potato, John the Ripper, OpenVPN, PsExec, PuTTy, SOCKS5 proxy, SSH tunneling, and Nmap to complete this challenge.

Week 6: Bypassing Network Defenses
This week, I had to infiltrate three difference target machines that had different network defenses. It varied from anti-virus to firewalls to Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS). The solution required Metasploit and Magic Unicorn to generate a payload that the victim machine would run that allowed to perform command injection. I created a test environment to run the test payload on in VMWare Workstation. The test enviornment consisted of three Window 10 machines, a Vyos Router, and a Snort IDS.

Week 7: Code-Level Vulnerabilities
We were given four programs to expose their vulnerabilties with memory leaks, stack smashing and buffer overflow. I had to consider possible preventive measures such as Address Space Layout Randomization (ASLR) and canary values. I exploited these vulnerabilites through the anaylsis of the arbitrary C code, and then performed a memory leak attack, buffer overflow attack, and a stack smashing attack which I had it run a remote shell on a port of my choice.

Week 8: Reverse Engineering and Malware Anaylsis
We were given malware to anaylize its static and dynamic effects on a victim machine. I used tools such as WhatIsRunning, RegShot, Procmon, Lord PE, PEid, OllyDbg, Ida Pro, FakeDNS, HWxD to perform static and dynamic anaylsis. I had to complete an overview of what the malware did in terms of the packer used, the DLLs created/modified, and significant registry key creations/modifications.

Week 9: Wireless Fundamentals
We were given a scenerio where we had to intercept packets between a web server and a user and perform ARP poisoning. We had to modify the website without the owner of the web server knowing. I used ettercap and its filtering abilities to modify the website in wireless transit.

SquadUp is a Pokemon team recommendation engine suited for both the casual and competitive audience. The goal of our app is to suggest complete and balanced Pokemon teams to the user.





Put in 0-5 Pokemon of your current team and 0-6 optional Pokemon of the opponent team, and our app will suggest the best Pokemon to fill your team in order to make it balanced as well as counter the opponent team if applicable. SquadUp also gives you the recommended natures and movesets for these Pokemon, as well as the breakdown of the offensive and defensive type coverage of the suggested teams, as well as provides a stat breakdown for each Pokemon to show the validity of our results

Casual Need: This app satisfies the need of a casual Pokemon player who wants suggestions on what Pokemon they need in order to help them complete the mainstream Pokemon games. In addition, it can also help casual players find a balanced team goes well with their favourite Pokemon.

Competitive Need: This app satisfies the need of the competitive Pokemon player who may need suggestions on a good balanced team that can beat a specific opponent team. In addition, it can also help competitive players find either offensive, balanced or defensive teams for specific Pokemon leagues.
